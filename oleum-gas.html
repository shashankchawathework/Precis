M.C. Mehta v. Union of India (Oleum Gas Leak) (1987)

Citation: AIR 1987 SC 1086

Bench: Supreme Court of India (P.N. Bhagwati, Misra Rangnath, G.L. Oza, M.M. Dutt, K.N. Singh)

Background:

Following a hazardous oleum gas leak from a factory in Delhi, public interest litigation was filed by M.C. Mehta against the Union of India seeking closure of the hazardous units and compensation for victims affected by the disaster.

Issues:

The Court examined the scope of the Supreme Court's jurisdiction under Article 32 to grant compensation and sought to define the measure of liability of enterprises engaged in hazardous or inherently dangerous industries for accidents causing death or injury.

Judgement:

The Supreme Court ruled that enterprises engaged in hazardous activities owe an absolute non-delegable duty to ensure safety. They are held strictly and absolutely liable for damages caused by accidents without exceptions for negligence. The principle of absolute liability was established, extending the rule in Rylands v. Fletcher to fit modern industrial conditions.

Key Takeaways:

  • Expanded the ambit of Article 32 for enforcement of fundamental rights, including compensation.
  • Established the principle of absolute liability for hazardous industries.
  • The measure of compensation should reflect the magnitude and capacity of the enterprise to have a deterrent effect.
  • Judicial activism in environmental and public health law protection.

Significance:

This judgment is a milestone in Indian environmental jurisprudence and industrial liability, underscoring the responsibility of hazardous industries to prevent harm and compensate victims adequately, shaping future public interest litigations.
