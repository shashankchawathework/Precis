Rylands v. Fletcher (1868)

Citation: UKHL 1, (1868) LR 3 HL 330

Court: House of Lords

Background:

The defendants constructed a reservoir on their land to power a mill, but beneath their property were old, disused mining shafts connected to the plaintiff's adjoining land. When the reservoir was filled, water broke through the shafts and flooded the plaintiff’s mine. The plaintiff sued for the damages caused by the escape of water.

Issues:

Whether a person who brings, collects, and keeps something potentially dangerous on their land is strictly liable if it escapes and causes damage, regardless of negligence.

Judgement:

The House of Lords held the defendants strictly liable for damages. The court established the rule that a person who brings onto their land anything likely to do mischief if it escapes must keep it at their peril. If it escapes and causes damage, the person is prima facie liable, regardless of the care taken.

Key Takeaways:

  • Established the rule of strict liability in tort law.
  • Liability applies for non-natural use of land if something escapes and causes foreseeable harm.
  • Defences include plaintiff's default, act of God, or consent, but not mere absence of negligence.
  • Foundation for future doctrines of strict and absolute liability in tort law worldwide.

Significance:

Rylands v. Fletcher is a foundational case for the principle of strict liability in tort. It is central to modern environmental and industrial liability law, holding those who bring hazardous substances onto their land liable for resulting harm, even without proof of negligence.
