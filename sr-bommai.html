S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994)

Citation: 1994 AIR 1918, 1994 SCC (3) 1

Bench: Kuldip Singh, P.B. Sawant, K. Ramaswamy, S.C. Agrawal, Yogeshwar Dayal, B.P. Jeevan Reddy, S.R. Pandian, A.M. Ahmadi

Background:

The case arose from the controversial use of Article 356 (President's Rule) in several Indian states. It examined the constitutional framework relating to the Center-State relationship and the scope of judicial review of President's Proclamation dismissing elected governments.

Issues:

Whether the dismissal of state governments under Article 356 was constitutionally valid and the extent to which such proclamations are subject to judicial scrutiny.

Judgement:

The Supreme Court laid down important guidelines limiting the scope of Article 356, emphasizing constitutional morality, and holding that the powers under Article 356 are subject to judicial review though exercised with restraint. It held that the President's satisfaction is justiciable, and arbitrary dismissal of state governments would be unconstitutional.

Key Takeaways:

  • Strengthened federal principles and autonomy of states within the Indian Union.
  • Established that Proclamation under Article 356 is subject to judicial review.
  • Emphasized constitutional morality's role in Centre-State relations.
  • Set guidelines for proper exercise of President’s power under Article 356.

Relevance in the Legal Field:

The judgment remains a cornerstone on federalism in India and delineates the limits of central power over states, influencing the political and legal landscape concerning state governance and the use of emergency provisions.
