Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978)

Citation: AIR 1978 SC 597

Bench: 7-Judge Bench

Background:

Maneka Gandhi challenged the impounding of her passport by the government, arguing it violated her fundamental rights to personal liberty and travel. The case extensively interpreted Article 21 of the Constitution.

Issues:

Whether the "right to life and personal liberty" under Article 21 includes the right to live with dignity and whether any procedure depriving such liberty must be just, fair, and reasonable.

Judgement:

The Supreme Court held that Article 21 guarantees a broad protection of life and liberty beyond mere physical existence. The Court ruled that any procedure depriving a person of liberty must be just, fair, and reasonable, and the State must respect principles of natural justice, including the right to be heard before depriving such liberty.

Key Takeaways:

  • Expanded the interpretation of Article 21 to include dignity and personal autonomy.
  • Introduced the requirement of substantive due process in Indian constitutional law.
  • Emphasized the interconnectedness of fundamental rights including Articles 14 and 19.
  • Ensured natural justice rights such as the right to hearing before administrative actions affecting fundamental rights.

Significance:

This landmark ruling transformed Indian constitutional jurisprudence by broadening the fundamental rights spectrum and establishing higher standards for state action affecting personal liberty. It remains foundational for protecting civil liberties.
