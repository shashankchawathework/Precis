Donoghue v. Stevenson (1932)

Citation: [1932] UKHL 100, [1932] AC 562

Court: House of Lords

Background:

The appellant, Donoghue, consumed ginger beer bought by a friend which contained a decomposed snail, leading to severe gastroenteritis. She sued the manufacturer, Stevenson, for negligence, despite no contract between them.

Issues:

Whether a manufacturer owes a duty of care to ultimate consumers even without a contractual relationship.

Judgement:

The House of Lords established the modern principle of negligence law, holding that a manufacturer owes a duty of care to the end consumer to ensure products are free from defects which could cause harm.

Key Takeaways:

  • Foundation of the modern law of negligence.
  • Established that duty of care extends to third parties not in contract with the manufacturer.
  • Introduced the 'neighbour principle' outlining who is owed care.
  • Emphasized protection of consumer health and safety.

Significance:

This landmark judgement transformed tort law by establishing manufacturer liability for consumer safety, influencing legal systems worldwide and shaping product liability laws.
