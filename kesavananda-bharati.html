Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973)

Citation: AIR 1973 SC 1461

Bench: 13-Judge Bench (Largest in Indian Judicial History)

Background:

Swami Kesavananda Bharati, head of a religious mutt in Kerala, challenged the Kerala government’s land reform legislation, arguing that it violated his fundamental rights under the Constitution. The case evolved into a major constitutional debate about the extent of Parliament’s power to amend the Constitution under Article 368.

Issues:

Whether Parliament has unlimited power to amend the Constitution, including Fundamental Rights, or whether there are inherent limitations on its amending power.

Judgement:

The Supreme Court, by a 7–6 majority, held that while Parliament has wide powers to amend the Constitution, it cannot alter its “basic structure.” This ruling limited Parliament’s authority to prevent it from destroying the Constitution’s essential features such as the rule of law, separation of powers, and judicial review.

Key Takeaways:

  • Established the Basic Structure Doctrine.
  • Ensured that the Constitution’s core principles remain protected.
  • Maintained a balance between parliamentary supremacy and constitutional supremacy.

Significance:

The judgment is considered the cornerstone of Indian constitutional law. It upholds the supremacy of the Constitution and acts as a safeguard against authoritarian amendments, ensuring that India remains a democratic republic governed by the rule of law.
