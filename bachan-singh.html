Bachan Singh v. State of Punjab (1980)

Citation: AIR 1980 SC 898

Bench: Constitution Bench

Background:

The case examined the constitutional validity of the death penalty under Indian law. It questioned whether the imposition of capital punishment violated the right to life and personal liberty under Article 21 of the Constitution.

Issues:

Whether death penalty is constitutional, and if so, under what circumstances it may be imposed without violating constitutional rights.

Judgement:

The Supreme Court upheld the constitutionality of the death penalty but introduced the 'rarest of rare' doctrine, ruling that capital punishment should only be awarded in the 'rarest of rare' cases where the alternative option of life imprisonment is unquestionably foreclosed.

Key Takeaways:

  • Death penalty is constitutional under Article 21, but to be used sparingly.
  • Introduced the 'rarest of rare' principle as a guideline for awarding capital punishment.
  • Emphasized the importance of judicial discretion and consideration of mitigating circumstances.
  • Stressed the need for consistency and fairness in sentencing to avoid arbitrariness.

Significance:

This verdict remains a pivotal ruling in Indian criminal law, shaping how death penalties are applied by establishing safeguards against arbitrary imposition and promoting judicial caution in capital sentencing.
