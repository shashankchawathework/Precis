Vishaka & Ors. v. State of Rajasthan & Ors. (1997)

Citation: AIR 1997 SC 3011

Bench: 3-Judge Bench (Chief Justice J.S. Verma, Sujata Manohar, B.N. Kirpal)

Background:

This case arose from a brutal gang-rape of a social worker in Rajasthan, which exposed the absence of legislative safeguards for women against sexual harassment at the workplace. A group of NGOs filed a PIL seeking protection for working women and the enforcement of fundamental rights in light of international human rights standards.

Issues:

Whether the lack of legislation on workplace sexual harassment violated women's rights to equality, freedom, and dignity under Articles 14, 19, and 21 of the Constitution, and whether the Supreme Court can frame binding guidelines to fill the legislative vacuum.

Judgement:

The Supreme Court held that sexual harassment of women at workplaces violates fundamental rights under Articles 14, 19, and 21. In the absence of legislation, the Court laid down the "Vishaka Guidelines" making it mandatory for all employers to provide safe working environments, set up redressal committees, and follow preventive and remedial measures for sexual harassment, which would be binding until a law is enacted by the legislature.

Key Takeaways:

  • Recognized workplace sexual harassment as a fundamental rights violation.
  • Established the Vishaka Guidelines as binding law until formal legislation.
  • Mandated redressal mechanisms and employer responsibility for prevention and punishment.
  • Used international norms and CEDAW as interpretive tools in Indian constitutional law.

Significance:

This judgment became a crucial foundation for the Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace (Prevention, Prohibition and Redressal) Act, 2013 and is frequently cited for gender justice and protection of vulnerable workers. It demonstrates the Supreme Court’s proactive approach in bridging legislative gaps for the protection of human rights and dignity.
